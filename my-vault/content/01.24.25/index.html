    went back to new jersey to celebrate moms bday and learn how to make dumplings dumpling recipe

    #1. prepare the cabbage

    • fill up a pot with water and dunk leaves of white napa
    • squeeze out the water using paper towels. feel the juice and water squirm on your hands.
    • use a long slender knife to dice the cabbage
      • i learned how to do this in middle school when they made me work in the cafeteria, mom told me. mom was sent to middle school - 8 rmb per month. her dad asked to borrow boarding money. her mom couldnt read or write but stressed the importance of education
      • whoever make dumpling will give to each other
    • she chides me for the rekless way i cut. fold your fingers inwards and dice it quickly. or if you want to do it the other way - she holds the knife by its base and begins hacking
      • it makes a nice sound. is ther a sound for this in chinese? um. just like qie qie qie #2 combine w ground chicken
    • no one else is using organic chicken like this, my mom says proudly. i dump it into the pot and wrinkle my nose at the side - never quite visually accustomed to the brain-like texture of ground chicken. she hands me a chopstick. start stirring i do it hesitantly, kindly, respectful of this dead chicken which was processed a second time and now a third time by my hand. she cracks a golden-yolked egg and spoons some chicken builloin. wow, this is like, some triple chicken action. is there a word for this? i ask earnestly, thinking of oyakodon. “san bei ji?”
    • she laughs and grabs the chopsticks and stirs at a speed 3x my own. the mystery meat becomes luscious, almost appetizing thanks to the (whats a word for the liquid part of an egg yolk. amniotic fluid?)

    #3 aromatics

    • quick chop some scallion and throw that it in. i marvel at the scent of fresh green onion, a stark cry from the wilted leftovers i threw in my egg mush the night before. it’s pungent and clear and addicting.
    • pour some wine, the xiaoxing kind, on top of the green-speckled mush. gets rid of the meat smell. i went to 99 ranch after a week of being sick and i could immediately smell the oil around me. it made me sick. her tongue is attuned to this meat smell in a way mine isnt.
    • a long, she asks dad, whos doing kalisthentics while watching chinese tv via roku - should we add sesame oil? of course.
    • finally, dash of white pepper and salt. not too much salt - this is what the sauce is for
    • admire your work. final state of chicken mush. in another world this is forbidden tartare, some delectable component of omakase. resist the urge to taste - this is what a test dumpling is for. #4 technique
    • one year we made homemade skin and i watched someone push their livelihood into the flour while every ingrate clutched their stomach and complained of hunger. better to use store-bought instead. it’s the north that makes their skin fresh.
    • you want to da zhi. make it da zhi. da zhi… like.. da “straight”? no, no like fold it.
    • set up your assembly line: meat, skin, a small cup of water. place the wrapper on your hand, spoon a little bit of meat filling. take two fingers and wet it before tracing the edges of the circular skin. fold in half and pinch: think of pleating or pie crust as you press into the gluten on the edges and push another part of wrapper on top, repeating all the way through.
    • the name shui jiao belies the cooking technique - just dump it in some water, right?
    • not quite. mom starts boiling water and explains - when the water boils, add dumplings in, then add cold water, then boil water again, then add water again, then wait for it to boil, then take it out.
    • wait what? can you say it again?
    • she complies and i sweat by the sweating pot. sweat sisters. i follow the instructions, despite my bleek context window. in the final stage the dumpling rises. she sends me back with the meat filling and wrappers (in a freezer tight bag, dw) and i hobble onto the nj transit. there’s also a gift of homemade wontons - this is what we actually eat in shanghai. i’ve already tried some of her wontons recently, when she was away and clutching my stomach in hunger i slurped up parcel after parcel, eyes swimming with the taste.
