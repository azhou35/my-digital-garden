    there are aunties packaging pig trotters and toddlers running around and fooling with the shopkeepers. this is a not a food court but a community space. a day care. these spaces emerged from necessity and adaptation. in the 80s, as manhattan’s chinatown grew increasingly expensive and expensive, chinese immigrants saw opportunity in flushing. these entrepreneurs began converting old retail spaces into food halls - a practical choice to cut expenses with low overhead costs for vendors and shared seating. in the process, they accidentally recreated the communal atmosphere of asia’s street markets.

    my parent’s journey in america began a stone’s throw away from flushing. after immigrating from china in the late 80s, their first landing zone was manhattan, and they raised their eldest daughter in forest hills. they’d shuttle her to dance classes, load up on dumplings at White Bear, pick up malaxianguo from the food courts.

    but I grew up in a different universe: Central Jersey, where culture meant fake tans and Italian women with over-gelled hair. at our local mall the only Chinese option was Panda Express - in some counterproductive act of rebellion, I begged for taco bell instead. no shared tables, only individual booths. We found our rhythm through weeknight stops at the Italian grocer, weekend trips to seafood festivals. while i yawned and groaned through chinese school, my parents practiced their English on orders of rigatoni and lobster rolls. in doing so, they traded the comfort of community for the comfort of control.

    it’s a calculation instinctive to immigrants: cultural belonging versus their children’s opportunity. new jersey was the best place to raise you, and they say it so often i almost believe it. with every move they made they went further from their roots and closer to the american dream. manicured lawns and strip malls and vehicular freedom.

    shanghai, i stood in a six-story mall food court, watching a robot glide between pristine tables with a tray of bubble tea. in this foreign utopia you can’t pay with card, let alone cash - every transaction is via WeChat Pay. with rows and rows of clean food stalls and pristine polished tables, the food courts here exude efficiency. the air smelled of nothing, a stark contrast from the constant smog of air pollution outside.

    skyscraper shopping malls have surmounted an old playground, the xiao chi stands have turned into third wave cafes. the city has moved along. in the past ten years a dizzying array of mega buildings have struck shanghai’s skyline, and many buildings have a feeling of being new but unused. spaces that arise out of design instead of necessity carry a special emptiness to them. when it’s almost too easy to find a seat at the food court, they lose the human messiness that makes them feel alive. isn’t it funny, the fronts we design as masks that hide our true selves?
