    # our systems are invisible and inept models of the world

    We perceive our systems as broken. In reality, we have made them invisible and modeled them incorrectly.

    Infrastructure powers our world - the X Y Z. These systems self-organize and control processes in a self-regulating loop. Infra is unsexy - when we look at a building, we first admire the craft of the architecture and design, and rarely consider the supply chain logistics and utility grids that birthed it to being. It’s a feature not a bug: our power lines and transportation systems are designed to be unnoticed.

    Even the software we depend on everyday is backed by physical DCs in NOVA, networking cables, etc. yet we have no conception that every inference call to ChatGPT or Google query triggers a careful orchestration of X Y Z. Abstraction widens the gap: we have software-level expectations - instant gratification - to utilities and services with real world constraints - e.g. the powerlines that give us reliable internet.

    Software has softened us with a friction-less life. Instant response. Infinite retries. We then project those expectations onto our daily lives - expecting instant trains and constantly operational busses. But these systems operate under real-world constraints.

    The gap is widened as we optimized systems for efficiency. In construction projects we cut corners and squeeze out costs, chosen optimization over resilience. As a result, systems become tightly coupled and have no room to fail. But efficiency cannot eliminate risk - it merely pushes it downstream.

    The lynchpin of these sequence of active choices is entropy: the notion that systems drift toward disorder unless energy, attention, and feedback are deliberately applied. There are two headwinds here: life is getting more chaotic at the same time our systems are becoming more tightly coupled. Our worsening climate situation means more hurricanes/earthquakes/storms to strain our systems, and globalization and progress has made intrasystem interdependence such that when when one failure point fails, the effects travel fast. A disruption in one place now ripples across industries elsewhere.

    And this is why it feels like our world is falling apart: the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway is crumbling beneath our feet; flight delays cascade thanks to outdated air control systems. The truth is that our bridges and airports are beyond their expected life span, and yet we cannot sustain the impact of taking these out of our system.

    Collapse looks sudden, but the damage has been accumulating quietly for years.
